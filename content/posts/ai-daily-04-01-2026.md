### When Source Code Becomes a 'Friend'
This morning, the AI community is buzzing over what appears to be a major leak: the full source code for **Claude Code** (Anthropic's terminal-based agent) was allegedly exposed via an accidental source map shipment in their npm registry. 

But as the day is April 1st, a closer look at the "leaked" logic reveals a salt string: `"friend-2026-401"`. 

#### The Punchline: Agentic Pets?
The "leaked" code includes references to 18 species of "AI friends" (including capybaras and ducks) that react to your coding in real-time. While clearly a high-effort April Fools feature, the reaction to the leak highlights a very real anxiety for 2026: **Agentic Security.**

#### Why This Matters for Solopreneurs
Whether this specific leak is a prank or a "controlled reveal," it underscores the primary risk of the agentic era: **Observability vs. Privacy.**

1.  **Local Context Risk**: When you run agents like Claude Code or OpenClaw, they have access to your local files. A real leak of the *orchestration* logic could expose how models are instructed to handle sensitive data.
2.  **The 'Undercover Mode' Myth**: One of the "leaked" features was an "Undercover Mode" that strips AI attribution from commits. Prank or not, this is a feature many developers are actually asking for—and one that raises massive ethical questions about the provenance of code.

#### Actionable Workflow for Today
Don't let the pranks distract you from your security hygiene. If you are using local AI agents:
- **Audit your .gitignore**: Ensure your `.env` files and SSH keys are strictly excluded from agentic scans.
- **Use Source Maps Wisely**: If you're shipping npm packages or web apps today, double-check that your production build *strips* source maps unless you want your logic to be the next "leak."

The line between a helpful "capybara coworker" and a security vulnerability is thinner than we think.
