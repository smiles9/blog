Over the last year, most AI headlines have focused on **models**: bigger, faster, smarter.

But if you look at what founders, engineers, and power users are actually excited about in early 2026, a different theme keeps surfacing:

> We are moving from **"talking to a model"** to **"working with an agent".**

Today's brief looks at what that means in practice and why it matters for people building small, lean, AI-powered projects.

---

### 1. From Chatbots to Agents

A **chatbot** answers questions.  
An **agent**:

- Understands your goal
- Decides which tools or APIs to call
- Takes multiple steps
- Keeps state and adjusts when things change

Recent product launches across the ecosystem – from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and open-source projects – point in the same direction:  
**"Agentic" workflows** that can:

- Read and write files
- Operate a browser
- Call external APIs
- Orchestrate multiple sub-tasks on your behalf

For example:

- Instead of asking a model, *"Write me an email to follow up with this lead"*, you ask an agent, *"Make sure all warm leads from last week get a polite follow-up today"* – and it drafts, personalises, and schedules those emails for you.

---

### 2. Why This Matters for Individuals and Small Teams

For big companies, agents mean automation at scale.

For individuals and small teams, they mean something even more personal:

- Offloading repetitive digital chores  
- Having a "second brain" that remembers context  
- Turning vague intentions into concrete actions

In practice, this looks like:

- An agent that keeps track of all documents related to a project and can answer questions like, *"What did we agree with X client about delivery timelines?"*
- A calendar + email agent that protects your focus time by rescheduling low-priority meetings automatically.
- A research agent that can scan 20 open tabs and give you a 1-page summary of what matters.

Instead of treating AI like a clever autocomplete box, you start treating it like a junior colleague.

---

### 3. The Real Competition: Who Owns the Workflow?

In the "model race", you can swap one provider for another.

In the **"agent race"**, switching costs go up:

- Once your workflows, tools, and data are wired into a particular agent platform, changing becomes harder.
- The platform that best understands your preferences, past actions, and business logic becomes very sticky.

This is why so many companies are rushing to:

- Ship agent frameworks
- Integrate with as many third-party tools as possible
- Build ecosystems around their agents

For users, the key is to:

- Stay flexible – avoid locking everything into a single proprietary platform too early
- Experiment with agents that can export data or run locally when possible
